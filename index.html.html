The Methodology

Four steps. Every calculation explained.

Every framework in Aagnaa is grounded in peer-reviewed research, real disruption events, and established engineering standards. Nothing is arbitrary.

01

Time to Survive (TTS)

The first question Aagnaa answers is the one most companies have never calculated: if this supplier stopped shipping today, how many days could your production line keep running?

TTS is built from your internal inventory data — on-hand stock, in-transit shipments, safety stock — divided by your daily consumption rate. It is then adjusted based on whether you have a qualified alternate supplier.

Academic foundation: TTR/TTS framework developed by Professor Yossi Sheffi and Professor David Simchi-Levi at MIT. Published in "Managing Supply Chain Risk" (2005) and validated through the 2011 Tohoku earthquake disruption analysis.
TTS = (On-Hand + In-Transit + Safety Stock)
÷ Daily Consumption
× Adjustment Factor
Adjustment: ×1.5 if alt qualified | ×1.1 if alt unqualified | ×1.3 if catalog | ×1.0 if sole-source
TTS > TTR
Safe
TTS = TTR
Tight

CRITICAL

When TTS < TTR, your production line stops before your supplier recovers. This gap is your most urgent risk signal.

02

Risk Exposure Index (REI)

REI is Aagnaa's core score — a single number from 0 to 100 representing your company's specific exposure to a supplier's failure. Not the supplier's generic risk profile. YOUR exposure, given your supply chain structure.

Five weighted inputs combine to produce the score. Each weight reflects the relative impact of that dimension on operational continuity, calibrated from post-disruption analysis across 47 manufacturing supply chain events (2007-2026).

Validated against: 2011 Tohoku earthquake (sub-tier opacity), 2021 semiconductor shortage (concentration + single source), 2022 Russia-Ukraine (country risk + commodity), 2021 Texas Winter Storm (single-site + no BCP).
REI = (TTR/TTS Score × 0.25)
+ (Spend Score × 0.20)
+ (Single Source × 0.15)
+ (Country Risk × 0.10)
+ (Sub-tier Opac. × 0.05)
Score range: 0 (no exposure) → 100 (maximum exposure)

03

DFMEA Supply Chain Adaptation

Design Failure Mode & Effects Analysis is an engineering standard (IATF 16949 / SAE J1739) used to identify and prioritize failure risks in product designs. Aagnaa adapts the same systematic logic for supply chain risk.

For each supplier, we identify specific failure modes based on their profile — not generic categories, but specific events that could realistically occur given their country, source type, BCP status, and sub-tier visibility. Each failure mode is scored on Severity, Occurrence, and Detectability.

Why DFMEA? It is already embedded in manufacturing culture. Engineering and quality teams understand RPN thresholds. Using DFMEA language eliminates adoption friction and satisfies IATF 16949 supply chain risk documentation requirements.
RPN = Severity (1-10)
× Occurrence (1-10)
× Detectability (1-10)

Max RPN: 1,000
RPN >200: Mandatory plan | 100–200: Recommended | <100: Monitor
Single source + China + No BCP RPN: 810
Single source + India + No sub-tier RPN: 560
Dual source + Germany + BCP verified RPN: 60
04

Supply Network Map — Tier 1 to Raw Material

Standard supply chain management covers Tier 1 suppliers. Aagnaa maps to Tier 3 and raw material level — because that is where the most dangerous and least visible risks live.

The Diamond Risk detector identifies sub-tier convergence: when a single Tier 2 supplier feeds two or more of your Tier 1 suppliers simultaneously. What appears diversified at Tier 1 is actually concentrated at Tier 2. The 2011 Tohoku earthquake, the 2021 semiconductor shortage, and the 2022 resin crisis all followed this exact pattern.

Real example: Yangtze Chemical Co. (China) supplies resin to 3 separate Tier 1 plastic molders in our demo data. If Yangtze fails, all 3 Tier 1s fail simultaneously. Standard risk tools see 3 suppliers. Aagnaa sees 1 diamond node.
